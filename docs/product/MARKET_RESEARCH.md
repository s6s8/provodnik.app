# Market Research: Provodnik

## 1. Executive summary

`Provodnik` should enter the Russian tours and excursions market not as another generic listing site, but as a lower-commission marketplace focused on:

- custom traveler requests;
- budget-based group formation;
- under-digitized regional supply;
- trust and operational discipline comparable to major incumbents.

The market appears concentrated around a small number of large aggregators, especially `Tripster` and `Sputnik8`, while a meaningful share of local demand and supply still flows through fragmented channels such as Telegram, phone calls, social media, classifieds, and personal networks.

This creates room for a product that organizes the fragmented tail of the market rather than trying to out-catalog incumbents.

## 2. Core market insight

The key opportunity is not just lower commission.

The deeper opportunity is that the market is still structurally messy:

- a large part of local supply is weakly digitized;
- communication is often manual and chaotic;
- group formation is poorly systematized;
- travelers with constrained budgets are underserved;
- many requests are custom and do not fit fixed listing flows well.

This suggests that a `demand-first marketplace` can occupy a meaningful niche.

## 3. Competitor snapshot

### Tripster

What official public materials show:

- Tripster is a large incumbent with broad geographic supply and strong depth in Russian destinations.
- It operates as a marketplace, not a classic tour operator.
- It enforces strong marketplace discipline:
  - best price rule;
  - fast response expectations;
  - visible response-time metrics;
  - anti-circumvention behavior before payment;
  - review and reputation mechanisms.
- It already incorporates attestation requirements for guides in Russia.

What this means:

- Tripster is strong on trust, conversion, and supply quality control.
- It is difficult to beat head-on with a similar listing-first product.

### Sputnik8

What official public materials show:

- Sputnik8 is also a large incumbent with wide coverage and a large guide base.
- Public guide-facing rules state a commission of `24% and more`, with a minimum of `200 RUB` per ticket.
- It relies on response speed, booking confirmation discipline, rating quality, and payment enforcement.
- It standardizes key marketplace mechanics such as cancellation and refunds.

What this means:

- Sputnik8 validates the marketplace model strongly.
- Its public commission level creates room for a lower-take alternative.

## 4. Competitive gap

The strongest gap is not “better listings.”

The strongest gap is:

- traveler-created requests;
- joining travelers into groups by budget and dates;
- flexible supply response from guides;
- regional and long-tail offers that are not consistently digitized;
- a cleaner workflow than Telegram threads and manual calls.

This is the part of the market that appears less directly owned by incumbents.

## 5. Why Provodnik can exist

`Provodnik` has a viable reason to exist if it is built around a different market motion:

- incumbents are listing-first;
- `Provodnik` should be request-first plus listing support;
- incumbents monetize broad catalog demand;
- `Provodnik` should monetize fragmented, custom, and budget-sensitive demand;
- incumbents already control trust basics;
- `Provodnik` must match trust basics, then differentiate on liquidity for group and custom demand.

This is a wedge strategy, not a frontal attack.

## 6. Pricing and monetization implication

Lower commission is commercially meaningful, but only if matched by real demand and trust.

Recommended positioning:

- incumbents: high-control marketplaces with higher take rates;
- `Provodnik`: lower-commission marketplace targeting `10% to 15%`.

However:

- lower commission alone is not a moat;
- lower commission without trust can attract low-quality supply;
- lower commission becomes compelling only when paired with new demand capture.

## 7. Risks

### Marketplace cold start

- guides do not join without demand;
- travelers do not stay without liquidity.

### Disintermediation

- guides and travelers may move off-platform after first contact;
- incumbents already treat this as a core structural problem.

### Trust and safety

- reviews, moderation, refunds, and guide verification are mandatory, not optional;
- high-risk excursions can create legal and reputational exposure.

### Regulation

- guide attestation in Russia is already part of the category reality;
- compliance cannot be deferred indefinitely.

### Price erosion

- unrestricted bargaining can reduce service quality and damage brand trust.

## 8. MVP implication

The MVP should not be a thin directory of tours.

It should contain the minimum system needed to create trusted transactions:

- guide profiles with identity and attestation fields;
- ready-made tour listings;
- traveler requests with budget, dates, region, and group size;
- group-join mechanics;
- fast response workflow between guide and traveler;
- deposit or prepayment logic;
- ratings and reviews;
- cancellation and refund rules;
- moderation and dispute handling.

This scope is justified because incumbents already validate the importance of trust and operational discipline, while the request-and-group flow is the clearest differentiation.

## 9. Market entry recommendation

Do not launch as “all tourism in Russia” in practice.

Recommended entry:

- start with a narrow regional wedge;
- use trusted local supply;
- focus on formats where group formation obviously reduces price friction;
- prove request-to-booking liquidity before scaling breadth.

The first success metric is not traffic.

The first success metric is repeatable marketplace liquidity in a narrow segment.

## 10. Bottom line

The research supports building `Provodnik`, but only under a clear strategic constraint:

Do not build a cheaper copy of `Tripster` or `Sputnik8`.

Build a lower-commission, trust-aware marketplace for:

- custom demand;
- group assembly;
- budget-sensitive travelers;
- under-digitized regional supply.

That is the part of the market most likely to give the product a real wedge.
